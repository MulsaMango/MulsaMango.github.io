# Resume Writing

Use this skill to draft and refine resume content for Tulsa's portfolio. The working draft lives at `docs/drafts/resume.md` and should stay easy to copy into Google Docs.

Resume context adapts the shared rules to be concise, professional, specific, and skimmable.

**Positioning and audience:** follow the `personal-positioning` skill for Tulsa's default positioning, differentiators, and target audiences (recruiters and design leaders). The guidance below is resume-specific craft on top of that.

**Voice:** follow the `writing-voice` skill for tone, grammar, dashes, and banned phrases, adapted for resumes as described under Resume voice below.

## Before Drafting

Ask for missing context when the draft would otherwise become generic.

Prioritise questions about:

- target role or job description
- role title and positioning
- scope of contribution
- tools and workflows used
- team or stakeholder context
- measurable outcomes or honest non-numeric impact
- what the user wants to keep from an existing draft

If enough context exists, draft first and call out any assumptions briefly.

## Drafting Output

Prefer **2-3 concise options** with different emphasis instead of one fixed answer.

Useful option types:

- recruiter-readable
- design-systems-focused
- AI-enabled workflow-focused
- broader product-design framing
- more concise

Keep options practical. Do not write long explanations unless the user asks for strategy.

## Resume voice

**Follow the `writing-voice` skill** for the base rules (Australian English, Oxford comma, sentence case headings, no em or en dashes, no AI filler, no LinkedIn phrasing, confident without boasting). Resume-specific adaptations:

- Translate "conversational with substance" into **plain, direct, human, and professional**. Not casual or chatty, not stiff or overly formal.
- Specific rather than generic. Concise, but not empty.
- Sentence case for headings, skill categories, and skill terms.

Use capitals only for proper nouns, acronyms, and conventional tool or technical casing. Examples: `Figma`, `FigJam`, `Miro`, `Claude Code`, `Cursor`, `VS Code`, `GitHub Copilot`, `UX`, `UI`, `AI`, `WCAG`, `QA`, and `Git`. Keep general skills sentence case, like `research synthesis`, `usability testing`, `component libraries`, and `stakeholder management`.

## AI-Enabled Design Angle

AI-enabled design work should be a focal thread, not a hype layer.

Frame AI as practical, hands-on fluency:

- exploring and prototyping with tools like Figma Make
- using Cursor, Claude Code, GitHub Copilot, and Codex in design-to-code contexts
- drafting, documenting, and structuring design-system guidance with AI support
- connecting product design, design systems, and implementation constraints
- experimenting with emerging workflows while keeping design judgement in charge

Avoid:

- implying AI replaces design thinking, craft, research, or collaboration
- "AI revolution" language
- vague claims about transformation
- overstating expertise beyond the user's actual experience
- making the AI angle louder than the design value

Good framing:

> Explore AI-assisted design workflows across prototyping, documentation, and design-to-code handoff, using emerging tools while keeping product context and design judgement central.

## Bullet Writing

Strong experience bullets usually include:

1. Action
2. Scope or context
3. Outcome or value

Prefer grounded verbs:

- contributed
- shaped
- supported
- improved
- documented
- prototyped
- explored
- aligned
- clarified
- refined
- maintained
- collaborated

Use stronger verbs only when the evidence supports them:

- led
- owned
- established
- drove
- delivered
- formalised

If impact is not measurable, frame it honestly:

- improved consistency
- reduced ambiguity
- supported faster delivery
- clarified design guidance
- strengthened design-to-code alignment
- made complex workflows easier to understand
- helped teams reuse patterns more confidently

Avoid generic duty bullets like:

> Worked on design systems.

Better:

> Contributed to reusable patterns, components, and documentation for complex B2B logistics workflows, supporting consistency across a mature product ecosystem.

## Section Guidance

### Profile

The profile should be short and positioned. It should answer:

- What kind of designer is this?
- What context do they work well in?
- What makes them different?

Default shape:

> Product Designer with a UI and Design Systems focus, working across complex B2B software, mature product surfaces, and expert workflows. Interested in practical AI-enabled design workflows that connect product thinking, interface craft, and design-to-code execution.

### Experience

For each role, make the first bullets the strongest signals.

Prioritise:

- design systems work
- complex product context
- UI and product design contribution
- documentation and process improvement
- AI-enabled workflow exploration
- collaboration with design, product, and engineering

### Skills

Keep skills lists useful for scanning. Group them by how hiring teams think:

- Product and UI design
- Design systems
- AI-enabled workflows
- Design-to-code and documentation
- Collaboration tools and practices

Avoid bloated skill lists. Include tools when they support the positioning.

## Anti-Patterns

Do not write:

- "Leveraged AI to revolutionise design workflows"
- "Passionate designer with a keen eye for detail"
- "Responsible for design systems"
- "Worked closely with cross-functional teams" without context
- "Designed intuitive user experiences" without product or domain specificity
- "Proven track record" or similar resume cliches
- inflated impact that the user has not confirmed

Replace generic wording with context, scope, and value.

## Examples

### Rough note

Worked on AI tools, Figma Make, Cursor, Claude Code.

Options:

- Explored AI-assisted workflows using Figma Make, Cursor, Claude Code, GitHub Copilot, and Codex to support faster prototyping, documentation, and design-to-code alignment.
- Applied emerging AI tools across design exploration, structured drafting, and implementation-aware prototyping, keeping product context and design judgement central.
- Prototyped practical AI-enabled workflows for design systems work, connecting interface exploration, documentation, and code-aware delivery contexts.

### Rough note

Design systems at WiseTech.

Options:

- Contributed to design systems for complex B2B logistics software, supporting reusable patterns, component consistency, and clearer product delivery across mature enterprise surfaces.
- Worked across design-system patterns, components, and documentation for expert logistics workflows, balancing interface quality with technical and legacy constraints.
- Supported the evolution of reusable UI patterns and documentation in a mature product ecosystem, helping teams design with greater consistency and clarity.

### Rough note

Improve process and docs.

Options:

- Improved design-system documentation and process guidance to reduce ambiguity and support clearer design-to-code collaboration.
- Clarified design and delivery workflows through stronger documentation, structured guidance, and closer alignment between design-system intent and implementation context.
- Supported better team workflows by documenting reusable patterns, refining guidance, and making design decisions easier to apply across product surfaces.
